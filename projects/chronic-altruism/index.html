Healthy Altruism

Genuine concern for others balanced with self-care. Motivated by empathy, personal growth, and openness. The giver maintains boundaries, can say no, and doesn't expect reciprocation. Positive outcomes for both parties. This is the baseline — where most people live.

🕊️

The Genuine Giver

Healthy → At-Risk

Authentically empathic, but at risk of burnout. Their giving comes from real compassion, not ego. The danger is depletion, not manipulation.

Core Motivation

  • Authentic empathy and compassion
  • Values-driven (personal growth, openness)
  • Derives meaning from reducing others' suffering

When It Becomes Pathological

  • Burnout and compassion fatigue (common in healthcare workers)
  • Self-neglect — skipping meals, sleep, own medical care
  • Inability to set boundaries without guilt
  • Identity becomes fused with the "helper" role

Research

  • Oakley (2012): burnout and depression as pathological altruism in caregivers
  • Linked to "unmitigated communion" (Fritz & Helgeson, 1998) — focus on others to exclusion of self

DSM-5 Adjacent: Dependent Personality Disorder features (need to be needed); Adjustment Disorders with burnout presentation

🔗

The Codependent

Vulnerable

Gives compulsively to maintain relationships. Confuses being needed with being loved. Their altruism is a survival strategy rooted in attachment wounds.

Core Motivation

  • Fear of abandonment and rejection
  • Self-worth contingent on being needed
  • Enmeshment — blurred self/other boundaries

When It Becomes Pathological

  • Enabling addictive or abusive behavior in others
  • Complete loss of identity outside the relationship
  • Chronic anxiety when not actively helping someone
  • Resentment cycle: over-give → feel unappreciated → guilt → over-give more

Research

  • Oakley (2012): codependency as canonical pathological altruism
  • Dear (2004): codependency linked to low self-esteem, external locus of control
  • High overlap with anxious attachment style (Bowlby)

DSM-5 Adjacent: Dependent Personality Disorder (301.6); features of Borderline PD (fear of abandonment, identity disturbance)

🎭

The Communal Narcissist

Dark Triad

Uses generosity as a stage. "I am the most giving person you'll ever meet." The altruism is real in action but hollow in motive — it serves grandiosity.

Core Motivation

  • Grandiose self-image maintained through communal domain
  • "I am the most caring, the most selfless, the most needed"
  • Seeks admiration for virtue, not for achievement

Key Behaviors

  • Public displays of generosity; less generous when unobserved
  • Keeps score — expects gratitude, recognition, reciprocity
  • Punishes perceived ingratitude (withdrawal, guilt-tripping)
  • Claims moral superiority in conflicts

Research

  • Gebauer et al. (2012): Communal Narcissism Inventory (CNI) — narcissistic self-motives satisfied through communal means
  • High subjective prosociality, low objective prosociality
  • Related to "self-sacrificing self-enhancement" (Pathological Narcissism Inventory)

DSM-5: Narcissistic Personality Disorder (301.81) — grandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathy. Communal presentation is a less-studied but validated subtype.

🐍

The Covert Narcissist

Dark Triad

Disguises control as care. Appears humble and self-sacrificing but weaponizes vulnerability. Their giving creates obligation and dependency in others.

Core Motivation

  • Control through obligation ("After everything I've done for you...")
  • Validation through victimhood ("I give and give and nobody appreciates me")
  • Maintains supply through dependency, not admiration

Key Behaviors

  • Unsolicited help that creates indebtedness
  • Martyr complex — suffering is performed, not private
  • Passive-aggressive withdrawal when not sufficiently appreciated
  • Conditional love disguised as unconditional sacrifice

Research

  • Wink (1991): covert/vulnerable narcissism — hypersensitivity, anxiety, defensiveness
  • Lower affective empathy despite outward displays of caring
  • "Altruistic narcissism" — prosocial facade concealing egoistic motivation

DSM-5: NPD (301.81) — "vulnerable" presentation not separately coded but clinically recognized. Overlaps with Passive-Aggressive (Negativistic) PD traits.

⚡

The Manic Altruist

Clinical — Bipolar

Explosive generosity during manic/hypomanic episodes. Gives away money, possessions, time recklessly. The altruism is genuine in the moment but driven by disinhibition.

Core Mechanism

  • Elevated mood + grandiosity + impulsivity = reckless generosity
  • Genuine desire to help, amplified by disinhibition
  • Poor judgment about consequences (financial ruin, relationship damage)

DSM-5 Manic Episode Criteria (Overlap)

  • Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity → "I can fix everyone's problems"
  • Excessive involvement in activities with painful consequences → buying sprees, giving away possessions
  • Increased goal-directed activity → taking on everyone's burdens simultaneously
  • Decreased need for sleep → staying up all night helping someone

Research

  • DSM-5: Bipolar I (296.xx) — "giving away possessions" listed as manic criterion example
  • Studies show manic patients exhibit impulsive generosity but also higher rejection of unfairness (altered social decision-making)
  • Zahn-Waxler (1984): children of bipolar parents show heightened distress at others' suffering

DSM-5: Bipolar I Disorder (296.xx), Bipolar II (296.89) — Criterion B7: "Excessive involvement in activities that have a high potential for painful consequences"

♟️

The Machiavellian Helper

Dark Triad

Strategic generosity. Every act of kindness is a chess move. They give to create leverage, build alliances, or position for future extraction.

Core Motivation

  • Power accumulation through social capital
  • Calculated reciprocity — always tracking the ledger
  • Long-game manipulation disguised as friendship

Key Behaviors

  • Targeted generosity — helps the "right" people strategically
  • Collects favors, calls them in at optimal moments
  • Appears warm and prosocial to observers
  • Zero genuine altruism when no audience or strategic value exists

Research

  • Machiavellianism negatively correlated with general altruism (Bereczkei et al., 2010)
  • Uses prosocial behavior as "social camouflage" (Jonason & McCain, 2012)
  • Instrumental helping: prosocial acts serve reputation management

DSM-5: No direct diagnosis. Overlaps with Antisocial PD (301.7) features — deceitfulness, exploitation. Not all Machiavellians meet ASPD threshold.

Bipolar Spectrum

Grandiose Self-Image
Inflated sense of ability to help. "I alone can save them." Temporary in mania, persistent in NPD.
Impulsive Generosity
Giving away money, possessions, time without considering consequences. DSM-5 Criterion B7.
Boundary Dissolution
Taking on others' problems as your own. Enmeshment accelerated by elevated mood states.
Identity Inflation
The "savior" identity feels real during episodes. Depressive crash brings guilt about overpromising.
Cycle of Overcommitment
Mania: promise everything. Depression: can't deliver. Guilt → compensatory hyper-giving on next cycle.

Dark Triad

Narcissistic Grandiosity
Stable (not episodic) inflated self-worth. "I am extraordinary at caring for others." Communal narcissism.
Performative Generosity
Giving is a stage. Diminishes in anonymous contexts. Expects audience and applause.
Control Through Obligation
Creates dependency. "After everything I've done..." Weaponizes the recipient's gratitude.
Idealize → Devalue Cycle
Shower with generosity → withdraw when supply dries up. Mimics bipolar cycling but is relational, not neurochemical.
Moral Self-Image
Core identity built around being "the good one." Threats to this image trigger narcissistic rage or collapse.

DSM-5 Diagnostic Intersections

Pathological altruism isn't a standalone DSM-5 diagnosis — but it overlaps with features of several. Click to expand criteria with altruism-relevant items highlighted.

Narcissistic Personality Disorder

301.81 (F60.81)
Communal narcissism satisfies grandiosity through prosocial means. "I am the most giving person alive" is NPD expressed through altruism rather than achievement.
  • Grandiose sense of self-importance → "No one helps like I do"
  • Preoccupied with fantasies of unlimited power → savior fantasies
  • Believes they are "special" and unique
  • Requires excessive admiration → for their generosity specifically
  • Sense of entitlement → expects gratitude and reciprocity
  • Interpersonally exploitative → creates obligation through giving
  • Lacks empathy → performs empathy, doesn't feel it deeply
  • Often envious or believes others are envious
  • Arrogant, haughty behaviors → moral superiority

Bipolar I Disorder — Manic Episode

296.xx (F31.x)
Manic episodes produce altruistic behavior through grandiosity and impulsivity. "Giving away possessions" is explicitly listed as a criterion example.
  • Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity → "I can solve everyone's problems"
  • Decreased need for sleep → staying up to help others
  • More talkative than usual → compulsive advice-giving
  • Flight of ideas → jumping between people's problems
  • Increase in goal-directed activity → taking on multiple causes simultaneously
  • Distractibility
  • Excessive involvement in activities with painful consequences → buying sprees, giving away possessions, reckless financial generosity

Dependent Personality Disorder

301.6 (F60.7)
The codependent altruist: gives compulsively to maintain attachment. Their generosity is a survival strategy against abandonment.
  • Difficulty making everyday decisions without advice/reassurance
  • Needs others to assume responsibility for major areas of life
  • Difficulty expressing disagreement due to fear of loss of support → can't say no to requests
  • Difficulty initiating projects due to lack of self-confidence
  • Goes to excessive lengths to obtain nurturance and support → over-gives to be needed
  • Feels uncomfortable or helpless when alone
  • Urgently seeks another relationship when one ends
  • Unrealistically preoccupied with fears of being left to take care of themselves

Antisocial Personality Disorder

301.7 (F60.2)
The Machiavellian helper: strategic prosociality as manipulation. Not all instrumental helping meets ASPD threshold, but the mechanism is the same — people as tools.
  • Failure to conform to social norms
  • Deceitfulness — using aliases, conning for personal profit/pleasure → prosocial facade
  • Impulsivity or failure to plan ahead
  • Irritability and aggressiveness
  • Reckless disregard for safety of others
  • Consistent irresponsibility
  • Lack of remorse → no guilt about using generosity as leverage
