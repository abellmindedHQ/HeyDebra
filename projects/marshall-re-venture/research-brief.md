## 2. WHY THIS THESIS WORKS

### Knoxville Multifamily: Structural Undersupply
- Knoxville has a **23,000-unit multifamily undersupply** as of 2025
- Ranked #5 nationally in emerging multifamily markets (Multi-Housing News)
- 96% occupancy, 1.8% employment growth
- Population projected to grow 15.2% by 2040
- Price per unit: ~$135,873 (down 4%, value-buy territory)
- Construction pipeline is surging now but expected to slow, creating a window

### Maryville/Alcoa: Spillover Growth
- Costco starting construction in Alcoa's Springbrook Farm (hundreds of jobs)
- Median home price $382K, projected to hit $407K in 12 months
- Infrastructure investment accelerating
- Still cheaper than Knoxville proper, with growing demand

### Grocery-Anchored Retail: The Recession-Proof Play
This is the strongest pillar of the pitch:
- During the Great Recession, grocery-anchored NOI was **slightly positive** while most RE tanked
- During COVID, these centers saw **5% NOI growth** over 3 years
- Cap rates: 6.4-6.8% nationally, compressing ~40bps from 2023 peak
- CBRE ranked grocery-anchored as **best expected performance** among all retail for next decade
- E-commerce resistant (grocery e-commerce share is small, stores serve as distribution hubs)
- Long-term leases (10-20 years), NNN structures, built-in rent escalations
- Limited new supply (construction costs + zoning) protects existing centers

### Western Plaza Specifics
- Anchored by The Fresh Market and CVS
- Also has Jos. A. Bank, Hard Knox Pizza, Lily Pulitzer, Tuesday Morning
- Located across from Sequoyah Hills (one of Knoxville's wealthiest neighborhoods)
- Strong demographics, high household income catchment area

## 3. HOW MARSHALL MAKES MONEY WITH LITTLE CAPITAL

This is the key question. Marshall's asset is his network and deal-sourcing ability, not his bank account. Here is how syndicators profit without significant personal capital:

### Fee Income (Earned Regardless of Investment Performance)

| Fee | Typical Range | When Earned |
|-----|--------------|-------------|
| Acquisition Fee | 1-3% of purchase price | At closing |
| Asset Management Fee | 1-2% of gross rents/year | Monthly/annually |
| Disposition Fee | 1-3% of sale price | At sale |
| Refinance Fee | 0.5-2% of new loan | At refinance |
| Organization Fee | ~1% of capital raised | At formation |
| Guarantee Fee | 0.5-2% of loan | For signing on debt |

**Example on a $5M acquisition:** 2% acquisition fee = $100K at closing. 1.5% annual asset management on $400K gross rents = $6K/year. These are real checks from day one.

### Promote (Carried Interest)
The big money. After LPs receive their preferred return (typically 7-8%), remaining profits are split. Common structure:

- First, LPs get 7-8% preferred return
- Then, profits split 70/30 or 80/20 (LP/GP)
- At higher return tiers, GP share can increase to 40-50%

Marshall as GP gets 20-30% of profits above the hurdle without putting up the capital. On a successful exit with 2x equity multiple, this can be substantial.

### Risk Minimization Strategies

1. **Co-GP with an experienced operator.** Marshall brings the network and capital. Partner with someone who has a track record managing multifamily/retail. Splits the GP promote but adds credibility and reduces execution risk.

2. **Start with 506(b).** No general solicitation, but allows up to 35 non-accredited investors. Perfect for Marshall's "friends and family" network approach. Lower legal costs than 506(c). Still needs a securities attorney and PPM.

3. **Grocery anchor = downside protection.** The Fresh Market isn't going anywhere. Long-term lease, essential service, wealthy catchment. Even if small tenants churn, the anchor provides baseline NOI.

4. **Separate LLCs per deal.** Don't cross-collateralize. If one deal goes sideways, it doesn't take down the fund. Each property is its own entity under the umbrella brand.

5. **Skin in the game (even symbolic).** Smart LPs want to see GP co-invest. Even $25-50K from Marshall in each deal signals alignment. He can fund this from acquisition fees on early deals.

6. **Property management outsource.** Don't self-manage. Hire a professional PM firm. The asset management fee covers Marshall's oversight role. Less operational risk, more scalable.

## 4. LEGAL REQUIREMENTS (TENNESSEE)

Marshall needs to get this right from the start:

- **SEC Regulation D filing** (Form D within 15 days of first sale)
- **Tennessee Blue Sky notice filing** ($500 fee)
- **Private Placement Memorandum (PPM)** drafted by a securities attorney
- **Operating Agreement** with GP/LP terms, waterfall, fee structure
- **Subscription Agreement** for each investor
- If accepting non-accredited investors (506b): additional disclosure requirements
- Securities attorney: budget $15-25K for initial fund formation docs

**Critical:** This is securities law. Marshall cannot wing this. One misstep and the whole thing unravels with rescission rights, penalties up to $10K/violation, and potential criminal exposure. Get the lawyer first.

## 5. THE THOUSAND OAKS QUESTION

One deal is in Thousand Oaks, California. This raises questions:

- The pitch is "keep Knoxville money in Knoxville." A California deal dilutes that narrative.
- California has additional regulatory requirements and higher operating costs
- Different market dynamics, different tenant base, different risk profile
- Consider: is this a strategic diversification play, or does it muddy the brand?

**Recommendation:** If the deal is strong, keep it. But frame it as "we invest where the returns are" rather than trying to force it into the Knoxville-local narrative. Or spin up a separate vehicle for out-of-state deals.
